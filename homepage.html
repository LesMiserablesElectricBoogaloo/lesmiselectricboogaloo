157 years ago, Victor Hugo released Les Miserables to the thunderous applause of French society. Today, we release Les Miserables Version 2.0: The Electric Boogaloo to the world. Aimed at modernising the meaning of Les Miserables, this album contains a variety of music, ranging from rock to pop, all related to thematic point of Les Miserables. Specifically, this album is centred around the thematic idea of the strength of relationships between people. Be it romantic love, familial love, or even just friendship, human bonding has the power to break social and economic restraints, to create happiness, and to ultimately redeem people. Kick back, relax, and enjoy!

The ultimate goal of this album is to show the universal power of human bonding. A major French novel 157 years ago and popular American music from the modern day may not have much in common, but they both show how human bonding can break restraints and redeem people. The ability to bond with others is a uniquely (and powerful) part of being human. It is a, “...grand thing... to be loved! What a far grander thing it is to love” (365)!

In the beginning of Fantine, when Jean Valjean reaches the little town of D----, Hugo uses diction to introduce the reader to a sorrowful world. He traverses throughout the street “at random”, “sad” and “humiliated”, followed by the townspeople's stares of “fear” and “distrust”. The “night”, with the “cold Alpine winds” howling in the background, is full “low clouds” (10-11). This mirrors the starting section of “We’re Finally Landing Home”. It consists of a slow tempo and moderately high pitches, which culminates into an overall sad tone. However, as the music progresses, a positive counter-melody slowly begins to develop. More positive diction is introduced as well. When Bienvenu opens the door to let Valjean in, Valjean’s face is described to have instantly lit up by the “fire”. As they feast inside on, “a soup, a few figs, green cheese”, the light of “two silver candlesticks” fill up the room (17-18). The shift in diction and tone from sadness into happiness emphasises the power of the priest’s generosity and respect towards Valjean. The bonds between people are ultimately based off such feelings.

Throughout most of this section, Hugo uses diction to create a sad tone around Fantine. Similar to the section, Inkwell Hell maintains a heavy and dreary tone. The song achieves this through, having a slow feeling pace. One note breaks-per bar in the drum beat slows the feel of the song. The song only has a few instruments which helps maintain a dreary tone. In the beginning of the text, Fantine is a “pretty blonde” with “gold” on “her head” and “pearls in her mouth” (42). Fantine’s situation degrades into poverty and desperation as she sells some of her teeth for medicine for her child, leaving “blood stains” and a “dark cavity” (66) in her mouth. She develops “a slight dry cough” and her arms becomes “hot” (64). She, “has nothing left of what she had been” (67). Despite this degradation, Fantine still loves her child dearly. The final description of Fantine is a contrast to her usual descriptions. Right before her death, when Fantine thought she would see Cosette, she “beamed with joy” and ”light up her whole countenance” (125). This is similar to the contrast in Inkwell Hell, in which a fluid solo develops, contrasting the repeated monotone background chords. This contrast in the tone surrounding Fantine shows the power of love. Fantine’s love for her child is very powerful, almost more powerful than reality. Love is a powerful feeling.

The idea of Let Her Go by Passenger matches the idea of submission that comes from multiple relationships formed between characters in Les Miserables, specifically between Monsieur Gillenormand and Marius and between Jean Valjean and Cosette.

Gillenormand and Valjean have similar roles to Marius and Cosette; they are both overprotective and dominating over their children. In the song, the line, “…never to touch and never to keep ‘cause you loved her too much and you dive too deep” parallels the story, in which both Gillenormand and Valjean, as the song puts it, “dived too deep” with their relationship with their children, dictating their children for their own benefit. In attempt to keep his love and obedience, Gillenormand prevents Marius from ever finding out about his dad and tries to dictate his political views while similarily Valjean tries to close off Cosette from society, mostly in an attempt to prevent her from leaving him to be with someone else.

However, both Monsieur Gillenormand and Jean Valjean eventually “let them go”, as the song says multiple times, and allow them to be free and happy by themselves. This submission is depicted in the song in the final lyric, which is sung in a defeated tone, “and you let her go”. This action of letting go of their beloved children characterises them as good fathers who put their children’s happiness before their own. Ultimately, both the song and the book represent the idea of submission and letting go in a relationship in order to allow for more freedom (and thus happiness) for the people involved.

The song Its Time begins with a character giving up on his good school life, saying “I get a little bit bigger but then I’ll admit, I’m just the same as I was”. He was “spent” and feels like “…giving the academy a raining check”, which means he wants to leave. He seems to realize that “...after all, this city never sleeps at night” and that everybody spends too much studying. If he drops out, he realises his life would be that “this road never looked so lonely”, and that everything he worked for would ultimately lead to unhappiness, he also says multiple times, “I’m never changing who I am”, showing that he does not have the courage to change who he is despite the situation. This is partly due to the fact that he “…don’t ever want to let you [the singer’s parents] down”.

After Javert is spared by Jean Valjean, he struggles between the fact that there is something above love, specifically the mercy and love Jean gives him, and the supreme authority of the law. He felt, “…that duty was growing weaker in his conscience” (530). Javert seems to question if, similar to the character in the song, that he will ever be able to change, as his primary fear is the two roads, “…before him… he saw two, and that terrified him” (530). This is symbolic of Javert’s dilemma. After much self-reflection, he accepts that “...there is then something more than duty” (531). Rather than changing his ways, Javert choose death. He rejects change, but because he realises his previous beliefs could not hold, he had to die.

The similar struggles felt by both Javert and the character in Its Time comes together to emphasize this rejection of change. Both show the power of relationships. For the character in Its Time, the power of his bonds to his parents makes him choose to stay in college, perhaps for the better as he avoided the “lonely” road. Although the love and mercy Jean shows to Javert ended in Javert rejecting this possibility of change, this still had a drastic effect on Javert, who spent his entire life living under his devout law. The power of love and human contact can move someone seemingly lacking emotion for their entire lives, to consider a new approach, and although this approach was death, it was a new approach nonetheless.

Viva La Vida by Coldplay tells a very similar story to that of Jean Valjean’s path taken throughout the entirety of Les Miserables. The song’s lyrics begin with a character seeming to retell his life story to Jesus, starting at the moment in which he was anointed “king”. As the story progresses, the character seems to discover more and more of the evil and sin of having authority, starting at the quote “I discovered that my castles stand upon pillars of salt, pillars of sand”. The reference to salt and sand is a biblical allusion to the idea that one’s basis for a kingdom is sinful and foolish. Salt represents standing on one’s past and sand represents standing on a false lifestyle. The character seems to have resolute acceptance of this sin when he realizes he’s “just a puppet on a lonely string… who would ever want to be king?” and accepts the fact that when he “ruled the world”, God was condemning him. The song concludes with, “Saint Peter won’t call my name… but that was when I ruled the world,” using the word “but” to show that he accepted that he was in sin when he ruled the world, but he is not king anymore, so he cannot just ignore such things.

This account is very similar to the story of Jean Valjean in Les Miserables. Valjean, much like the character of Viva La Vida, felt as if he “ruled the world” at times, and throughout his experience as “king”, or in his case, a rich mayor of a small town, he was always in fear of returning to his sinful self prior to being mayor. He always wanted to keep his castles from standing on pillars of salt or sand, that is, standing on his past (the salt), or of a false lifestyle (the sand). His primary motivation was “…to close the door on his past” (89), showing a similar fear of their past selves shared between Jean Valjean and the character in the song. Both Jean Valjean and the one in Viva La Vida end their journeys never directly claiming to have been redeemed from their fear of themselves and their past evil. However, both realise their past errors and have changed. For Jean Valjean, Hugo writes that “the light from the candlesticks fell upon him… some mighty angel was standing... awaiting his soul” (594), in order to show that he was ultimately forgiven, since the candlesticks were originally used to represent the saving of Jean Valjean by the Bishop. Meanwhile, in Viva La Vida, the character asks Jesus to “Be my mirror, sword and shield”, which alludes to another instance in the Bible (Ephesians 6:10-18), which ultimately refers to these defensive tools as the word of God, protecting one from the evil of the world, especially the sin coming from temporal reign.

This use of similar symbolism and allusions ultimately ties together the song Viva La Vida and the character Jean Valjean.
